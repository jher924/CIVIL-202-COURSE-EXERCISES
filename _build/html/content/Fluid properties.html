7.* [R] Calculate the specific weight, density and specific volume of air at 50 °C and absolute pressure of 3.4 bar. Given: R_air = 287 m2/(s2.K). {36 N/m3; 3.67 kg/m3; 0.273 m3/kg}

The values of \(R_{air}\), \(T\), and \(P\) are given. If we convert the temperature units to K and the pressure units to Pa, we have:

%%render param
T=50+273.15 #K
p=3.4*10**5 #Pa
R_air=287 #m2/(s2.k)
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} T &= 323.150 \; \;\textrm{(K)} &p &= 340000.000 \; \;\textrm{(Pa)} &R_{air} &= 287 \; \;\textrm{(m2/(s2.k))} \\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

The specific weight can be calculated as \(\gamma = \frac{g \cdot P}{R_{air} \cdot T}\)
The density is \(\rho = \gamma/g\)
The specific volume is \(\nu = 1/\rho\)

%%render long 3

gamma= (g*p)/(R_air*T) #N/m3
rho= gamma/g #kg/m3
v= 1/rho #m3/kg
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \gamma &= \frac{ g \cdot p }{ R_{air} \cdot T } \\&= \frac{ 9.810 \cdot 340000.000 }{ 287 \cdot 323.150 } \\&= 35.963 \; \;\textrm{(N/m3)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \rho &= \frac{ \gamma }{ g } \\&= \frac{ 35.963 }{ 9.810 } \\&= 3.666 \; \;\textrm{(kg/m3)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] v &= \frac{ 1 }{ \rho } \\&= \frac{ 1 }{ 3.666 } \\&= 0.273 \; \;\textrm{(m3/kg)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} P_{75_{C}} &= 39 \; \;\textrm{(kPa)} \\[10pt] P_{atm_{}} &= 101 \; \;\textrm{(kPa)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{dP} &= P_{atm_{}} - P_{75_{C}} \\&= 101 - 39 \\&= 62 \; \;\textrm{(kPa)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

4.*A square block has a contact area of 0.02 m2 with a table that is lubricated with a 0.1 mm thick layer of oil with viscosity 7 mPa.s. What force is required to move the block at a constant velocity of 1 m/s? {1.4 N}

If we convert the depth of the layer to m, and viscosity to Pa/s, we have the following given parameters:

%%render param
A=0.02 #m2
y=0.1 #mm
mu_oil=7*10**-3 #Pa/s
Delta_V= 1 #m/s
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} A &= 0.020 \; \;\textrm{(m2)} &y &= 0.100 \; \;\textrm{(mm)} &\mu_{oil} &= 0.007 \; \;\textrm{(Pa/s)} \\[10pt] \Delta_{V} &= 1 \; \;\textrm{(m/s)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

At a constant velocity, aceleration is zero, therefore: $\( \sum F=0\\ \)\( Force can be calculated as \)\( F-\tau A=0\\ \)\( Where \)\( \tau = \mu \frac {\Delta V}{\Delta y} \)$

%%render long 

Delta_y= y/1000 #m

tau= mu_oil*(Delta_V/Delta_y) #Pa

F= tau*A #N
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \Delta_{y} &= \frac{ y }{ 1000 } \\&= \frac{ 0.100 }{ 1000 } \\&= 0.000 \; \;\textrm{(m)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \tau &= \mu_{oil} \cdot \left( \frac{ \Delta_{V} }{ \Delta_{y} } \right) \\&= 0.007 \cdot \left( \frac{ 1 }{ 0.000 } \right) \\&= 70.000 \; \;\textrm{(Pa)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] F &= \tau \cdot A \\&= 70.000 \cdot 0.020 \\&= 1.400 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

5.**[R] A space 16 mm wide between two large plane surfaces is filled with oil with a viscosity μ = 0.18 N.s/m2. What force F will be required to drag a very thin plate of 0.4 m2 area between the surfaces at a speed of 0.25 m/s if the plate is positioned 5 mm from the bottom surface? {5.24 N}

%%render param
mu=0.18 #N.s/m2
V=0.25 #m/s
yplate=5/1000 #m
yoil=16/1000 #m
A=0.4 #m2
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \mu &= 0.180 \; \;\textrm{(N.s/m2)} &V &= 0.250 \; \;\textrm{(m/s)} &\mathrm{yplate} &= 0.005 \; \;\textrm{(m)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{yoil} &= 0.016 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &A &= 0.400 \; \;\textrm{(m2)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

To calculate the force required to drag the plate, the Newton’s equation of viscosity can be used:

\[\begin{split} \tau= \mu\frac{dV}{dy}\\ F=\tau A \end{split}\]

The total force is the sum between the force of the oil and the force of the plate

\[\begin{split} F_{oil}= \mu\frac{dV}{(y_{oil}-y_{plate})} A\\ F_{plate}= \mu\frac{dV}{dy} A \end{split}\]
%%render long
y_foil= yoil-yplate #m
tau_oil= mu*(V/y_foil) #N/m2
tau_plate= mu*(V/yplate) #N/m2
F_oil= tau_oil*A #N
F_plate= tau_plate*A #N
F= F_oil+F_plate #N
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} y_{foil} &= \mathrm{yoil} - \mathrm{yplate} \\&= 0.016 - 0.005 \\&= 0.011 \; \;\textrm{(m)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \tau_{oil} &= \mu \cdot \left( \frac{ V }{ y_{foil} } \right) \\&= 0.180 \cdot \left( \frac{ 0.250 }{ 0.011 } \right) \\&= 4.091 \; \;\textrm{(N/m2)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \tau_{plate} &= \mu \cdot \left( \frac{ V }{ \mathrm{yplate} } \right) \\&= 0.180 \cdot \left( \frac{ 0.250 }{ 0.005 } \right) \\&= 9.000 \; \;\textrm{(N/m2)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] F_{oil} &= \tau_{oil} \cdot A \\&= 4.091 \cdot 0.400 \\&= 1.636 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] F_{plate} &= \tau_{plate} \cdot A \\&= 9.000 \cdot 0.400 \\&= 3.600 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] F &= F_{oil} + F_{plate} \\&= 1.636 + 3.600 \\&= 5.236 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

6.**A space 10 mm wide between two large plane surfaces is filled with oil with viscosity μ = 0.18 N.s/m2. A very thin plate with a 0.5 m2 area is located halfway between the two surfaces. If a constant force of 10 N is applied the plate, at what speed will it be dragged out from between the surfaces? {0.278 m/s}

Given:

%%render param
A=0.5 #m2
L=5 #mm
mu =0.18 #N.s/m2
F= 10 #N
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} A &= 0.500 \; \;\textrm{(m2)} &L &= 5 \; \;\textrm{(mm)} &\mu &= 0.180 \; \;\textrm{(N.s/m2)} \\[10pt] F &= 10 \; \;\textrm{(N)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Force can be calculated as: $\( F=A\mu \frac {\Delta V}{\Delta y}\\ \)\( Where the total Area is \)1m^2\( because the area of one plate is \)A= 0.5 m^2$.

Solving for \(\Delta V\):

\[ \Delta V =F\frac {\Delta y}{\mu A_{total}} \]

Considering that \(\Delta y\) is equal to \(L\) in m:

%%render long
L_m= L*10**-3 #m
A_total=A*2
V= F*L_m/(mu*A_total) #m/s
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} L_{m} &= L \cdot \left( 10 \right) ^{ -3 } \\&= 5 \cdot \left( 10 \right) ^{ -3 } \\&= 0.005 \; \;\textrm{(m)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] A_{total} &= 1.000 \; \\[10pt] V &= F \cdot \frac{ L_{m} }{ \mu \cdot A_{total} } \\&= 10 \cdot \frac{ 0.005 }{ 0.180 \cdot 1.000 } \\&= 0.278 \; \;\textrm{(m/s)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

7.***[R] A square block weighing 1.1 kN and with side lengths of 250 mm slides down a 20 ° incline on a film of oil 6.0 micrometre thick. Assuming a linear velocity profile in the oil, what is the terminal velocity of the block? The viscosity of the oil is 7 mPa.s. {5.16 m/s}

Given:

%%render param
W=1.1*10**3 #N
theta=20 #°
dy=6*10**-6 #m
mu=7*10**-3 #Pa.s
L=250/1000 #m
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} W &= 1100.000 \; \;\textrm{(N)} &\theta &= 20 \; \;\textrm{(°)} &\mathrm{dy} &= 0.000 \; \;\textrm{(m)} \\[10pt] \mu &= 0.007 \; \;\textrm{(Pa.s)} &L &= 0.250 \; \;\textrm{(m)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]
%%render long

A= L**2 #m2
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} A &= \left( L \right) ^{ 2 } \\&= \left( 0.250 \right) ^{ 2 } \\&= 0.062 \; \;\textrm{(m2)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

At terminal velocity, the block is moving at a constant speed, meaning the net force acting on it is zero. By considering the forces along each axis, the following equation can be derived:

\[\begin{split} \sum F_x=0\\ F_\mu-Wsin\theta=0\\ F_\mu=Wsin\theta\\ F_\mu=\tau A\\ F_\mu=\mu \frac{dV}{dy} A\\ \mu \frac{dV}{dy} A=Wsin\theta\\ \end{split}\]
%%render long

dV= (W*sin(radians(theta))*dy)/(A*mu) #m/s
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \mathrm{dV} &= \frac{ W \cdot \sin { \left( \operatorname{radians} \theta \right) } \cdot \mathrm{dy} }{ A \cdot \mu } \\&= \frac{ 1100.000 \cdot \sin { \left( \operatorname{radians} 20 \right) } \cdot 0.000 }{ 0.062 \cdot 0.007 } \\&= 5.160 \; \;\textrm{(m/s)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

8.*** A hydraulic lift consist of a 280.00 mm diameter ram which slides in a 280.18 mm cylinder, the annular space being filled with oil having a kinematic viscosity of 0.00042 m2/s and a specific gravity of 0.86. If the rate of travel of the ram is 0.22 m/s, find the frictional resistance when 2 m of the ram is engaged in the cylinder. {1553 N}

If we convert the diameters of the hydraulic lift and the cilinder to m, we have the following parameters:

%%render param 4
d_lift= 280/1000 #m
d_cyl=280.18/1000 #m
nu= 0.00042 #m2/s
S=0.86 
Delta_vel=0.22 #m/s
h=2 #m
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} d_{lift} &= 0.2800 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &d_{cyl} &= 0.2802 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &\nu &= 0.0004 \; \;\textrm{(m2/s)} \\[10pt] S &= 0.8600 \; &\Delta_{vel} &= 0.2200 \; \;\textrm{(m/s)} &h &= 2 \; \;\textrm{(m)} \\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

The frition resistance can be calculated as \(F=\tau A\)

Where \(A=h \cdot \pi \cdot d_{lift}\) and \(\tau = \mu \frac {\Delta V}{\Delta y}\)

The dynamic viscosity can be calculated as \(\mu = \nu \cdot \rho\), where \(\rho = s \cdot \rho_{water}\)

%%render long 
rho= S*rho_water #kg/m3
mu= nu*rho #Pa.s
Delta_y= (d_cyl-d_lift)/2 #m

tau= mu*Delta_vel/(Delta_y) #Pa
A= h*pi*d_lift #m2
F= tau*A #N
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \rho &= S \cdot \rho_{water} \\&= 0.860 \cdot 1000 \\&= 860.000 \; \;\textrm{(kg/m3)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \mu &= \nu \cdot \rho \\&= 0.000 \cdot 860.000 \\&= 0.361 \; \;\textrm{(Pa.s)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \Delta_{y} &= \frac{ d_{cyl} - d_{lift} }{ 2 } \\&= \frac{ 0.280 - 0.280 }{ 2 } \\&= 0.000 \; \;\textrm{(m)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] \tau &= \mu \cdot \frac{ \Delta_{vel} }{ \Delta_{y} } \\&= 0.361 \cdot \frac{ 0.220 }{ 0.000 } \\&= 882.933 \; \;\textrm{(Pa)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] A &= h \cdot \pi \cdot d_{lift} \\&= 2 \cdot 3.142 \cdot 0.280 \\&= 1.759 \; \;\textrm{(m2)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] F &= \tau \cdot A \\&= 882.933 \cdot 1.759 \\&= 1553.337 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

9.*** A piston with diameter of 127 mm, height 152 mm and weight 93.4 N slides free in a lubricated vertical pipe. The clearance between the piston and the pipe is 0.0254 mm. If the piston decelerates at 0.64 m/s2 when its speed is 6.4 m/s, what is the viscosity of the oil? { 0.0065 N.s/m2 }

Given:

%%render param
d=127/1000 #m
h=152/1000 #m
W=93.4 #N
dy=0.0254/1000 #m
dV=0.64 #m/s2
a=0.64 #m/s2
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} d &= 0.127 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &h &= 0.152 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &W &= 93.400 \; \;\textrm{(N)} \\[10pt] \mathrm{dy} &= 0.000 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &\mathrm{dV} &= 0.640 \; \;\textrm{(m/s2)} &a &= 0.640 \; \;\textrm{(m/s2)} \\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]
%%render long

A= 2*pi*(d/2)*h #m2
m= W/g #kg
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} A &= 2 \cdot \pi \cdot \left( \frac{ d }{ 2 } \right) \cdot h \\&= 2 \cdot 3.142 \cdot \left( \frac{ 0.127 }{ 2 } \right) \cdot 0.152 \\&= 0.061 \; \;\textrm{(m2)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] m &= \frac{ W }{ g } \\&= \frac{ 93.400 }{ 9.810 } \\&= 9.521 \; \;\textrm{(kg)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

In this case, since there is a deceleration, the sum of forces along the axis must be set equal to mass times acceleration. Including all the forces in the equation, we have the following $\( \sum F=ma\\ F_\tau - W=ma\\ \)\( and \)F_\tau\( is: \)\( F_\tau=\mu \frac{dV}{dy} A\\ \mu \frac{dV}{dy} A - W =ma \)$

%%render long

mu= ((m*a+W)/(A*dV))*dy #N.s/m2
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \mu &= \left( \frac{ m \cdot a + W }{ A \cdot \mathrm{dV} } \right) \cdot \mathrm{dy} \\&= \left( \frac{ 9.521 \cdot 0.640 + 93.400 }{ 0.061 \cdot 0.640 } \right) \cdot 0.000 \\&= 0.065 \; \;\textrm{(N.s/m2)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

10.*** A journal bearing consists of an 80.0 mm diameter shaft in an 80.4 mm sleeve 120 mm long. The clearance space is filled with oil with a viscosity of 0.11 N.s/m2. Calculate the torque required to keep the shaft turning at 150 rpm. {0.417 N.m}

The given parameters are the following: (Note that the diameters and length were converted to meters).

%%render param

d_journal= 80/1000 #m
d_sleeve= 80.4/1000 #m
long_sleeve= 120/1000 #m
mu_filled=0.11 #Ns/m2
N_rpm=150 #rpm
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} d_{journal} &= 0.080 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &d_{sleeve} &= 0.080 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &\mathrm{long}_{sleeve} &= 0.120 \; \;\textrm{(m)} \\[10pt] \mu_{filled} &= 0.110 \; \;\textrm{(Ns/m2)} &N_{rpm} &= 150 \; \;\textrm{(rpm)} \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

The torque can be calculated as \(Torque = F \cdot r_{journal}\)

Where \(\tau= \mu\frac {\Delta V}{\Delta y}\) and \(V=\frac{r_{journal}\cdot2\cdot\pi\cdot N_{rpm}}{60}\)

%%render long
r_journal= (d_journal)/2 #m

V= (r_journal*2*pi*N_rpm)/60 #m/s

Delta_y= ((d_sleeve-d_journal))/2 #m

F= tau*pi*(d_journal)*(long_sleeve) #N

12.*[R] A glass tube with diameter 1.5 mm is dipped vertically in water. Calculate from basic principles how high the water will rise in the tube due to capillary action. Take the coefficient of surface tension to be 0.0730 N/m and the angle of contact as 10°. {19.5 mm}

%%render param
d_tube=1.5/1000 #m
sigma=0.0730 #N/m
theta=10 #°
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} d_{tube} &= 0.002 \; \;\textrm{(m)} &\sigma &= 0.073 \; \;\textrm{(N/m)} &\theta &= 10 \; \;\textrm{(°)} \\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

equating the lifting force created by surface tension to the gravity force, we obtain the following:

\[\begin{split} \sum F =0 \\ F_{\sigma}-W=0\\ F_{\sigma}=\sigma cos(\theta) \pi d\\ W = \frac {\pi d^2 h\rho g}{4}\\ \end{split}\]

Replacing the \(F_{\sigma}\) and \(W\)

\[ \sigma cos(\theta) \pi d-\frac {\pi d^2 h\rho g}{4}=0 \]

Calculating \(F_{sigma}\) and solving for \(h\):

%%render long 4
theta_rad = radians(theta) #rad

F_sigma= sigma*pi*d_tube_m*cos(theta_rad) #N
h= F_sigma*4/(d_tube**2*rho_water*g*pi) #m
\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \theta_{rad} &= \operatorname{radians} { \left( \theta \right) } \\&= \operatorname{radians} { \left( 10 \right) } \\&= 0.1745 \; \;\textrm{(rad)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] F_{\sigma} &= \sigma \cdot \pi \cdot d_{tube_{m}} \cdot \cos \left( \theta_{rad} \right) \\&= 0.0730 \cdot 3.1416 \cdot 0.0015 \cdot \cos \left( 0.1745 \right) \\&= 0.0003 \; \;\textrm{(N)}\\[10pt] \\[10pt] h &= F_{\sigma} \cdot \frac{ 4 }{ \left( d_{tube} \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot \rho_{water} \cdot g \cdot \pi } \\&= 0.0003 \cdot \frac{ 4 }{ \left( 0.0015 \right) ^{ 2 } \cdot 1000 \cdot 9.8100 \cdot 3.1416 } \\&= 0.0195 \; \;\textrm{(m)}\\[10pt] \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

\(h\) in mm would be:

%%render long 4
h_=h*1000 #mm
\[ \begin{aligned} h_{} &= 19.5422 \; \;\textrm{(mm)} \end{aligned} \]

13.*A glass tube with diameter 1 mm is dipped vertically in water. Calculate from basic principles how high the water will rise in the tube due to capillary action. Take the coefficient of surface tension to be 0.0730 N/m and the angle of contact as 20°. {28 mm}

Given:

%%render param

To calculate the height of the water, it is necessary to perform a force balance along that axis, and include all the forces.
